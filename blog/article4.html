How to Wire a 240V Outlet for a Welder or EV Charger — Electrician Explains

I've been a journeyman electrician for over a decade, and one of the most common calls I get is some version of: "I just bought a welder (or an EV charger) and I need a 240-volt outlet in my garage." It's a straightforward job if you know what you're doing — and a dangerous one if you don't. So let me walk you through exactly how to wire a 240V outlet for a welder or EV charger the right way, to code, first time.

Fair warning: if you're not comfortable working inside your electrical panel, hire a licensed electrician. There's no shame in that. A 240-volt circuit will kill you just as dead as anything else if you make a mistake.

Why You Need a Dedicated 240V Circuit

Most welders rated above 140 amps and virtually all Level 2 EV chargers need 240 volts. You can't just plug them into a regular 120V outlet — they'll either refuse to start or trip your breaker immediately. A dedicated 240V circuit gives the equipment the voltage and amperage it needs to run properly without sharing load with anything else on that circuit.

"Dedicated" is the key word here. Per the NEC, a 50-amp receptacle outlet on a branch circuit needs to be on a circuit where it's the only outlet — no piggybacking off your dryer or range circuit. One circuit, one outlet, one piece of equipment.

What Gauge Wire Do You Need?

For a typical 240V welder or 40-amp EV charger on a 50-amp breaker, you need 6 AWG copper wire. Specifically, you want 6/3 NM-B (Romex) for interior runs or 6/3 UF-B for any underground or exterior runs.

Here's the breakdown by breaker size:

  • 30-amp breaker: 10 AWG copper minimum
  • 40-amp breaker: 8 AWG copper minimum
  • 50-amp breaker: 6 AWG copper minimum
  • 60-amp breaker: 4 AWG copper minimum

These are based on the NEC 310.16 ampacity table for 60°C-rated conductors. If your run is longer than 50 feet, you should consider bumping up one wire size to account for voltage drop. On a 100-foot run, voltage drop gets real — your welder will arc poorly and your EV charger will throttle back.

Breaker Size — Getting It Right

Most 240V welders in the 200–250 amp output range draw between 40 and 50 amps at the plug. Most Level 2 EV chargers (like the Tesla Wall Connector or ChargePoint Home Flex) pull 40 amps continuous. Per NEC 210.21(B)(1), if the load is continuous (running for 3 hours or more — and EV charging absolutely qualifies), the receptacle rating can't be less than the branch circuit rating.

In practice, a 50-amp double-pole breaker is the standard for this type of circuit. It covers the vast majority of welders and EV chargers you'd install in a residential garage.

Choosing the Right NEMA Receptacle

This is where people get tripped up. There are different NEMA receptacle configurations, and using the wrong one is a code violation. For a 240V-only circuit (no neutral needed), the correct receptacle is:

  • NEMA 6-50R — 50-amp, 250-volt, 2-pole, 3-wire (two hots + ground). This is the standard for welders and many EV chargers.

Do not use a NEMA 14-50R (the one that looks like a dryer outlet) unless your equipment specifically requires a neutral conductor. A NEMA 14-50 has four prongs — two hots, a neutral, and a ground. A NEMA 6-50 has three prongs — two hots and a ground. Most welders use the 6-50. Most EV chargers can use either, but check your manual.

If you're hardwiring an EV charger (no plug at all), you don't need a receptacle — you wire directly into a junction box. But I generally recommend installing a receptacle anyway. It makes future equipment swaps much easier.

Running the Wire — Step by Step

Here's the actual process, simplified:

  1. Turn off the main breaker. Verify it's dead with a non-contact voltage tester. Then verify again with a multimeter. Trust me.
  2. Plan your route from the panel to the outlet location. Shortest path wins. Drill through studs where needed, use cable staples every 4.5 feet and within 12 inches of every box.
  3. Install a metal or plastic single-gang box at the outlet location. I prefer metal boxes for 240V circuits — they're sturdier and easier to ground.
  4. Pull your 6/3 cable from the panel to the box. Leave at least 12 inches of extra wire at each end — you can always trim, but you can't add.
  5. Wire the receptacle: Connect the two hot wires (black and red) to the brass terminals on the NEMA 6-50R. Connect the green or bare ground wire to the green screw. Cap off the white neutral wire if you're using 6/3 — it's not used on a 6-50 circuit.
  6. Wire the breaker: Land the two hot wires on the 50-amp double-pole breaker. Land the ground on the ground bar. If you have a white neutral, land it on the neutral bar (even if unused at the receptacle, it must terminate properly).
  7. Button everything up, restore power, and test the outlet with a multimeter. You should read 240V across the two hots and 120V from each hot to ground.

Grounding — Don't Skip This

Per NEC 250.122, the equipment grounding conductor for a 50-amp circuit must be at least 10 AWG copper. If you're running 6/3 NM-B cable, the bare ground wire inside is already sized correctly — it's 10 AWG. You're covered.

But here's where I see DIYers cut corners: they'll skip the ground entirely, or they'll use a two-wire cable and bond neutral to ground at the receptacle. That's not just a code violation — it's genuinely dangerous. A lost neutral on a grounded receptacle means the equipment chassis becomes energized. That's how people get electrocuted.

Run the ground. Every time. No exceptions.

NEC Code References You Should Know

If your local inspector asks (and they should, if you pull a permit), here are the key NEC sections that govern this installation:

  • NEC 210.21(B) — Outlet devices must have an ampere rating not less than the load to be served. For a single receptacle on a branch circuit, the receptacle rating must not be less than the rating of the branch circuit.
  • NEC 250.122 — Minimum size of equipment grounding conductors. For a 50-amp circuit, that's 10 AWG copper.
  • NEC 210.8(A) — GFCI requirements. As of the 2023 NEC, 240V outlets in garages now require GFCI protection. Check with your local jurisdiction — not all areas have adopted the 2023 code yet, but it's coming.
  • NEC 310.16 — Ampacity tables for conductors. This is your reference for wire sizing.

Always pull a permit for this type of work. A 240V circuit addition is not a "handyman special." If something goes wrong and you didn't permit it, your insurance company will have a field day denying your claim.

Final Thoughts

Wiring a 240V outlet for a welder or EV charger is one of the more straightforward electrical jobs you can do — but "straightforward" doesn't mean "casual." You're working with enough power to cause a fire or kill someone if the connections are wrong. Use the right wire gauge, the right breaker, the right receptacle, and ground everything properly.

If you follow the steps above and pull your permit, you'll have a clean, code-compliant 240V circuit that'll run your welder or charge your truck for years.
